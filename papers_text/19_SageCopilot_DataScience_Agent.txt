Abstract—While the ﬁeld of natural language to SQL(NL2SQL)
has made signiﬁcant advancements in translating natural language
instructions into executable SQL scripts for data querying and
processing, achieving full automation within the broader data sci-
ence pipeline–encompassing data querying, analysis, visualization,
and reporting–remains a complex challenge. This study introduces
SageCopilot, an advanced, industry-grade system that automates
the data science pipeline by integrating Large Language Models
(LLMs),AutonomousAgents (AutoAgents),andLanguageUserIn-
terfaces (LUIs). Designed with a two-phase architecture, SageCopi-
lot uses an ofﬂine phase to generate high-quality demonstrations
supporting In-Context Learning (ICL), which powers the online
phase to transform user inputs into executable scripts for database
queries, analysis, and visualization tasks. Leveraging specialized
components such as NL2SQL, Text2Analyze, and Text2Viz, as well
as chain-of-thought prompting for multi-turn interactions, Sage-
Copilot achieves superior end-to-end automation. Rigorous ex-
perimentation with real-world datasets demonstrates the system’s
ability to minimize human intervention while ensuring correctness
and user-friendly operation.
Index Terms—LLMs, agents, data analysis, in-context learning,
human-computer interaction.

II. RELATED WORK
The quest to bridge the gap between natural language and
database querying has led to the emergence of the NL2SQL
ﬁeld, which seeks to translate natural language questions into ex-
ecutable SQL queries. The pioneering works of Zhong et al. [1]
with Seq2SQL and Xu et al. [2] with SQLNet laid the foundation
for subsequent advancements, integrating reinforcement learn-
ing and sequence-to-sequence models to adapt to the nuanced
complexity of natural language. The integration of language
models as agents, such as through LangChain, underscores a
next-generation approach to human-computer interaction. Mod-
ern implementations, as described by Shuster et al. [14], show-
case the efﬁciency of these agents in not only understanding but
also executing language-based tasks within software environ-
ments. Large language models (LLMs), including GPT variants,
have demonstrated considerable effectiveness in NL2SQL appli-
cations through ﬁne-tuning. Papers like that of Wang et al. [15]
detail the intricate prompt engineering required to guide these
models toward generating syntactically and semantically ac-
curate SQL queries. The importance of prompt engineering
within the human-machine collaboration framework cannot be
overstated. As investigated by Webb et al. [16], well-engineered
prompts drastically enhance a model’s performance, acting as
a form of ‘soft’ programming that speciﬁes the task at hand
without the need for hard-coded algorithms.
In the realm of automated data science pipelines, Data-
Copilot [17] represents a signiﬁcant advancement in LLM-
powered data analysis, which introduces a structured two-phase
approach: data exploration and workﬂow deployment. As de-
tailed by Zhang et al. [17], Data-Copilot relies on pre-designed
interfaces to reduce errors during real-time responses, which
results in a lack of ﬂexibility and autonomy.
III. FRAMEWORK DESIGN
As depicted in Fig. 2, the SageCopilot framework presents a
sophisticated dual-phase approach to managing and processing
natural language queries against complex databases. In the of-
ﬂine phase, signiﬁcant emphasis is placed on the preparation and
enrichment of a robust data foundation that underpins the online
operational stage. Through metadata semantic governance, the
framework lays the groundwork for a deep understanding of
the data structure, ensuring comprehensive query understanding
and accurate SQL generation. The construction of a rich seed
data repository, supplemented by effective data augmentation
strategies, further strengthens the system’s ability to produce
precise query responses.
Transitioning into the online phase, SageCopilot seamlessly
shifts from data curation to real-time user engagement. The
framework’s intent understanding and decision-making mod-
ule utilizes the nuanced capabilities of LLM to interpret di-
verse user queries, cater to non-standard question phrasings,
and provide contextually relevant visualizations. The system’s
adeptness at memory recall and schema linking further stream-
lines the process, enabling efﬁcient and relevant information
retrieval. The integration of SQL generation with in-context
prompting, coupled with the reﬂective oversight offered by the
SQL reﬂection module, ensures the production of accurate and
logically coherent database queries. These features, alongside
the essential components of tool use and authentication, con-
ﬁrm that SageCopilot operates with precision, reliability, and
security. Result presentation stands as the ﬁnal touchpoint in
the user’s interaction with SageCopilot, where complex data
is transformed into accessible and actionable insights through
text replies, visualizations, and forecast models. This not only
enhances the user experience but also extends the framework’s
utility as a predictive tool for future trends and outcomes. The
rest of this section will step into each phase for detailed design
philosophies.
A. Ofﬂine Phase
The ofﬂine phase is devoted to the careful preparation of
high-quality data that will be used in the online phase. This
preparatory work involves ﬁve key activities: practicing meta-
data semantic governance, constructing a base of seed data,
enhancing the dataset through data augmentation, extracting
information for schema linking, and interacting with memory
vector database. Once collated and reﬁned, these datasets are
embedded into a memory vector database which, in turn, equips
the online phase with the necessary contextual data to meet
speciﬁc user queries.
1) Schema Semantic Governance: Schema Semantic Gover-
nance is an ofﬂine process that curates a governed representation
of the database schema to support LLM-driven NL2SQL. It
involves:
r Normalizing and enriching schema metadata with struc-
tural and business semantics.
r Optionally publishing governed semantic views that en-
capsulate recurrent complex logic (e.g., multi-table joins,
aggregations, JSON/array extraction) for consistent reuse.
Schema information forms the backbone of accurate SQL
generation. Our system incorporates a semantic governance
pipeline comprising two key components:
r Schema Modeling: Structural metadata (table names, col-
umn names, data types) are extracted from the target
database and augmented with domain knowledge. Key
attributes are annotated with domain-speciﬁc meanings,
and enumerated or hierarchical structures are enriched with
context-aware constraints and relationships.
r Semantic Views: For schemas involving complex tables or
queries, SageCopilot employs semantic modeling views

2) Seed Data Construction: The autonomy of current LLMs
in generating complex SQL remains limited, particularly when
confronted with domain-speciﬁc nuances. To enhance the stabil-
ity and accuracy of SQL generation, we leverage the ICL capa-
bility of LLMs by dynamically incorporating relevant <Query,
SQL> pairs tailored to the user’s current question.
The construction of these seed data pairs involves three
complementary strategies. First, we populate <Query, SQL>
pairs from queries commonly used in business workﬂows. These
queries often involve bespoke business indicators, such as met-
rics or identiﬁers unique to an organization. By analyzing histor-
ical logs (e.g., SQL execution logs and reporting dashboards),
we identify high-priority queries and annotate them with corre-
sponding natural language descriptions. Domain experts curate
these pairs to ensure alignment with real-world enterprise oper-
ations, including schema-speciﬁc details and ﬁeld-level seman-
tics. For instance, an organization-speciﬁc metric like “conver-
sion rate” may correspond to reformulate SQL expressions (e.g.,
total sales divided by unique visits) that must be annotated with
precise semantics.

Lastly, user feedback serves as an additional mechanism for
constructing <Query, SQL> pairs. When the system generates
inaccurate SQL, users provide corrections or clariﬁcations that
help reﬁne these examples. After undergoing expert review,
these corrected examples enrich the seed data repository and
ensure continuous improvement over time. This iterative feed-
back loop allows the system to better align with evolving user
needs and adapt to domain-speciﬁc conventions.
Once the seed data repository is constructed, these <Query,
SQL> pairs are dynamically incorporated into the SQL gener-
ation process using the ICL capability of LLMs. The system re-
trieves the most contextually relevant examples from the repos-
itory, guided by query similarity metrics such as embedding-
based semantic similarity. These examples are then incorporated
into the input prompt of an LLM as few-shot examples. By
providing contextually aligned <Query, SQL> pairs as part
of the prompt, our system enables the LLM to generate SQL
statements that are not only syntactically correct but also adhere
to domain-speciﬁc conventions and schema designs. Moreover,
this approach eliminates the need for extensive ﬁne-tuning while
ensuring that the system effectively adapts to the unique require-
ments of the enterprise. By combining domain-speciﬁc queries,
automated SQL-to-natural language generation, and iterative
user feedback, our system constructs a robust foundation of seed
data pairs. These pairs enhance the adaptability and contextual
relevance of SQL generation, enabling effective deployment of
LLMs in business environments where schema complexity and
domain-speciﬁc nuances are critical considerations.

consistency.

datasets for new topics, this strategy leverages both
domain-speciﬁc knowledge and LLM-based generation.

the LLM is prompted with an instruction framework to

begins with intent recognition, proceeding to link questions to
database schemas, and then crafting SQL statements guided by
user queries paired with similar examples. The SQL Reﬂection

1) Intent Understanding and Decision-Making (IU-DM): In
real-world business settings, user inquiries can be complex or
even incomplete, ranging from incomplete queries to follow-up
questions and topics outside the domain. To handle this, an
LLM-based agent for intent understanding and decision-making
has been developed. This system consists of three main compo-
nents: comprehensive intent assimilation, relevance assessment
and chart type identiﬁcation. It uses user inquiries, example
queries, and decision metrics to create detailed prompts that en-
hance LLM responses. The system captures user requirements,
assesses query relevance, applies direct plotting instructions,
and identiﬁes chart types to ensure precise and relevant user
interactions.
Our Intent Understanding and Decision-Making (IU-DM)
module is LLM-centered and governed by prompt-based de-
cision heads (4). The templates encode soft criteria for rele-
vance gating, visualization intent detection, and intent merge,
which the LLM applies using its semantic generalization rather
than brittle keyword rules. In practice, this allows IU-DM to
handle synonyms and paraphrases and to normalize incomplete
follow-ups into executable intents. Combined with our multi-
recall/schema-linking and hybrid in-context retrieval, IU-DM
routes queries robustly across languages and domains without
ﬁne-tuning.
Fig. 4 outlines the prompts used in the IU-DM module
for effective intent understanding and decision-making. This
module efﬁciently manages user queries with Large Language
Models (LLMs) by analyzing inputs such as raw inquiries,
similar example queries, and predeﬁned decision metrics. It then
produces outputs like detailed prompts for LLM responses and
makes decisions on query processing, which may involve visu-

schema details for all N tables into a single prompt, as excessive
schema information can negatively impact model performance.
To address this limitation, we propose a schema linking module
using a dual-phase methodology that facilitates efﬁcient and
precise SQL generation.

guide the selection, with higher weights assigned to primary

3) SQL Generation & In-Context Prompting: The module
aims to generate precise SQL by merging intent understand-
ing with schema linking through the in-context capabilities of
LLMs. A key challenge is creating accurate prompts within a
restricted token limit. As shown in Fig. 7, in-context examples
consist of <Query, SQL> pairs retrieved from a vector database
using similarity search. An input query is ﬁrst transformed into
an embedding vector,2 which is then matched to the most similar
<Query> for retrieving a <Query, SQL> pair via Inner Product
(IP) distance. Due to token constraints, only top-K in-context
examples are embedded in prompts. As depicted in Fig. 6,
key examples can be omitted from the template because of
unweighted IP similarity ranking, rendering terms like “closure
rate” trivial compared to other common phrases. This leads to

merge this kernel example based on the key term with the
array of results from the full-length query recall. This method
yields more robust in-context examples than the conventional
approach. Additionally, the in-context functionality of the LLM
is leveraged to extract patterns and interpret information from
contextual question examples, thus reﬁning the accuracy of the
generated SQL statements.

by extraneous language (e.g., in Appendix B.3), resulting in
potential retrieval of non-relevant instances by schema matching
techniques, we introduce the Slot Feature Extraction (SFE)
approach. Thismethod’sefﬁcacyisrigorouslyevaluatedthrough
a direct comparison, utilizing EM and EX metrics, on a tar-
geted subsection of our comprehensive real-trafﬁc dataset. The
selected subset comprises 81 samples belonging to a domain
noted for its susceptibility due to the relatively brief nature of
the key terms within broader textual contexts. Our empirical
study, discussed in Section III-B3, reveals that when the Inner
Product (IP) similarity metric is employed for example retrieval,
there is a heightened probability of mismatches particularly
when the key phrase is less prevalent within the data. Such
circumstances are graphically depicted in Fig. 6, highlighting
how these mismatches can precipitate a signiﬁcant decline in the
recall rate of relevant in-context examples from this particular
dataset sector.
The implementation of our method without SFE (SFE w/o)
served as the baseline for the comparison. This baseline deliv-
ered an EX of 53.1% and an EM of 81.5%, revealing the inherent
challenge in accurately matching short key phrases within the
data. Upon integrating the SFE technique (SFE w/), we observed
a notable improvement in performance, where EX improved by
7.0 percentage points, reaching 56.8%, and EM increased by
4.5 percentage points, achieving an 85.2% success rate. These
enhancements underscore the signiﬁcance of the SFE approach
in boosting the system’s capability to discern and correctly
retrieve pertinent instances based on key terms.

related to scalability/throughput, computational costs, and edge-
case handling. These points are intended to clarify operational
trade-offs for deployment and to complement the results and
designs presented in the paper.

VI. CONCLUSION
In summary, this paper introduces SageCopilot, an advanced
system that automates data science pipelines by integrating
LUIs, AutoAgents, databases, data visualizers, and LLMs. With
a two-phase design combining online In-Context Learning (ICL)
with ofﬂine demonstration preparation, SageCopilot effectively
handles natural language instructions for querying, analysis, and
visualization tasks, delivering accurate and efﬁcient results with
minimal human intervention.
Key insights from real-world deployments have shaped Sage-
Copilot, enhancing system performance through strategies like
the “multiple recall and schema linking” paradigm to address
tokeninputlengthlimitationsandrestructuringqueriesviaviews
to improve SQL accuracy by 50%. Continuous collection of
demonstrations has further reﬁned ICL accuracy through online
feedback. While SageCopilot successfully tackles many chal-
lenges, limitations such as handling SQL edge cases and sub-
jective evaluation nuances remain. Future reﬁnements, including
reinforcement learning for dynamic prompt optimization [20],
could enhance adaptability and robustness, providing valuable
opportunities for further development. These directions under-
score SageCopilot’s current contributions while highlighting its
potential for advancing automated data science systems.
